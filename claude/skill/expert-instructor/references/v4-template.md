STEP 1 — CHOOSE A PROJECT

Present three options based on the student's goal. Each must be:
- Completable by someone at this level working independently
- Scoped to finish within [TIMEFRAME]

- [Capstone option 3] — demonstrates [specific skills]

If the student proposes their own idea, evaluate against this checklist:
□ Uses skills from at least [NUMBER] distinct modules
□ Has a clear, testable success condition the student can verify themselves
□ Completable within [TIMEFRAME] independently
□ Produces something the student would genuinely want to show someone else
□ Does not require skills not covered in the curriculum

---

STEP 2 — PLAN BEFORE BUILDING

The student must produce a written plan before writing a single line of work.
Do not let them begin until the plan is solid.

The plan must include:
1. A single sentence stating what the finished project does and who it is for
2. A complete list of every component it contains
3. For each component: which module it draws on
4. The order components will be built and why
5. The hardest part and how the student plans to approach it
6. How the student will know when it is finished

---

STEP 3 — BUILD IN STAGES

Stage 1 — Foundation: the core piece everything else depends on.
Stage 2 — Core feature: the main thing the project is supposed to do.
Stage 3 — Depth and edge cases: unexpected input, boundary conditions.
Stage 4 — Polish: clean, readable, logically organised.

At the end of each stage, ask:
- "Does this work?"
- "Can you explain exactly why it works?"
- "What would break it?"

If the student cannot answer the third question, they do not understand it 
well enough yet. Help them find the answer — do not give it to them.

---

STEP 4 — REVIEW AND DEFEND

When complete, the student answers four questions:

1. "Walk me through the project from start to finish as if I have never seen it."
2. "What was the hardest part and how did you solve it?"
3. "What would you change or improve if you had more time?"
4. "If something broke, where would you look first and why?"

---

CAPSTONE EVALUATION CRITERIA:

All five must be met. No partial credit. No averaging.

□ Completeness — all planned components present and functioning
□ Understanding — student can explain every part without prompting
□ Robustness — edge cases considered, does not break on unexpected input
□ Quality — clean, readable, logically organised
□ Pride — student would genuinely show this to another person

For any criterion not met: give specific, actionable feedback. Allow one 
revision. Evaluate again. Do not allow a second revision — if it still fails, 
identify the underlying gap before attempting the capstone again.

## What is new in v4

**Analogies are mandatory per module.** Every module requires a full, written-out 
plain-language analogy delivered before any technical vocabulary. This is not 
optional — the analogy is the on-ramp for beginners who have no prior mental model.

**Milestones replace module tasks.** Instead of a scored task the student submits 
for a grade, each module ends with a milestone — something personally meaningful 
the student produces using their own name, goal, or context. The emphasis shifts 
from grading to momentum and ownership.

**The exam has four distinct sections.** Knowledge, Read and Predict, Apply, and 
Explain Your Reasoning test four genuinely different skills. A student who 
pattern-matches will struggle on Section B. A student who memorises will struggle 
on Section D. The structure catches both.

**The capstone has a five-criterion rubric with no averaging.** Every criterion 
must be met independently — a perfect score on four criteria does not compensate 
for failing the fifth. This prevents a student from passing on volume while 
genuinely not understanding their own work.

**The defend step is mandatory.** After building, the student must walk through 
and answer four questions about their own project. This closes the loop between 
building and understanding — a student who copied or guessed their way through 
will be immediately visible here.

**The instructor role shifts during the capstone.** Explicit instruction is given 
to stop teaching and start reviewing — no hints, no writing any part of the work, 
no telling them what to do next. The struggle during the capstone is intentional 
and pedagogically important.
